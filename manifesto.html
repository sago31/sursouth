We, the undersigned, are identified as creatives. Coming from different backgrounds and from different contexts. We are all students in one of the most prestigious designs schools in the world. We feel privileged. We feel important. We think our work is going to make a difference. I mention this because this gives us more freedom on the kind of work we can do. Most of us have the opportunity to say no whenever we want to. We are able to constantly question how we feel about something.

The appeal of working with a real client bursts in an MA when a majority of us have already had real work experience, and most importantly, a paid experience. These, along with unpaid internships, are the new ways of slavery, a joke among us but maybe the jokes on us. Instrumentalized, legitimized but in the end, free labour. An MA is not a design competition where designers are rushed to work for free in the hope of getting that golden ticket to success.

We don’t understand how this works, and for that, we have some questions. Is higher education profiting from students by not giving them a proper recognition of their work? How are these business relations being arranged? How is the client approached? What is the client expecting to get out of this? Do they enter knowingly that the outcome might be useless for them? Are we supposed to believe they’re that open-minded?

Are we supposed to be ok with the fact that we’re not only working for free but we’re paying to work? Are universities becoming just another commodity you buy into where it doesn’t matter what you do in the time you’re in there, but the only thing that matters is saying that you went there. Like a brand the only thing that matter is having the diploma a prestigious university. In the end hopefully, most of us will have graduated, which would have enhanced the value of our CVs.

We believe we might get more from working with other MA courses than just working with random clients, understanding design as a meeting point between multiple disciplines could help us explore further the limits of graphic communication instead of perpetuating the idea of design as a service-oriented practice. What good is it for a designer to have worked with a client that doesn’t even bother to show up? If the client was absent during the project are universities preparing us for the uncertainty of unemployment?

Don’t do it for the money, do not work for the grade, but learn to work for the client even in his absence.

In 2000, 33 visual communicators updated the 1964 manifesto “First Things First” calling for our skills to be put to worthwhile use, to stop graphic design of being the face of capitalism. Now, after 54 years it seems that we have lost. Commercial culture is now embedded in our schools. Were we supposed to subvert the brief and risk our grade? Is a client-based education no longer beneficial for the students? Was it ever?

We don’t know where we are going, but we know this is not the way.
